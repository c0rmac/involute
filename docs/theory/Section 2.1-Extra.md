

> **Theorem 2.2: The Intrinsic Infinite Series Representation**
>
> Let $(\mathcal{M}, g)$ be a locally symmetric Riemannian manifold and let $M \in \mathcal{M}$ be the frozen consensus point. For any $X$ within the injectivity radius of $M$, the probability density $p(X)$ relative to the flat Lebesgue measure of the tangent space $T_M\mathcal{M}$ is given by:
>
> $$p(X) = \frac{1}{Z} \exp\left( -\frac{\lambda}{\delta^2} d_g^2(X, M) + \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{m=2}^{N} \frac{1}{m!} \nabla^m_{v} \psi(0) \right)$$
>
> where $v = \text{Log}_M(X)$ is the tangent vector, $d_g(X, M)$ is the intrinsic Riemannian distance, and $\nabla^m_{v} \psi(0)$ denotes the $m$-th order directional derivative of the log-Jacobian $\psi(v) = \ln J(v)$ evaluated at the origin of the tangent space.

**Proof.**

1. **Baseline Tangent Density:** By Lemma 2.2, the equivalent density in the tangent space for a particle within the injectivity radius of $M$ is defined as $p(v) = \frac{1}{Z} \exp\left( -\frac{\lambda}{\delta^2} \|v\|_g^2 + \psi(v) \right)$, where $\psi(v)$ encapsulates the geometric volume distortion.
2. **Intrinsic Distance Substitution:** By the definition of the Riemannian logarithm map, the norm of the tangent vector $v = \text{Log}_M(X)$ equates to the geodesic distance $d_g(X, M) = \|v\|_g$. Substituting this into the density provides the primary energetic potential $-\frac{\lambda}{\delta^2} d_g^2(X, M)$.
3. **Maclaurin Expansion of the Log-Jacobian:** To evaluate $\psi(v)$ explicitly, we perform a formal Maclaurin series expansion of this scalar function evaluated at the origin of the tangent space, $v=0$.
4. **Zeroth-Order Term:** Because $M$ is the origin of Riemannian normal coordinates, the exponential map is locally an isometry, establishing $J(0) = 1$. Therefore, the zeroth-order term vanishes: $\psi(0) = \ln(1) = 0$.
5. **First-Order Term:** Furthermore, the local symmetry of the manifold $(\mathcal{M}, g)$ enforces that the first covariant derivative of the log-Jacobian vanishes identically at the origin, yielding $\nabla_v \psi(0) = 0$.
6. **Infinite Series Synthesis:** Consequently, the expansion of $\psi(v)$ possesses no zeroth- or first-order terms, reducing strictly to an infinite sequence of higher-order tensor derivatives evaluated at the origin: $\psi(v) = \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{m=2}^{N} \frac{1}{m!} \nabla_v^m \psi(0)$.
7. **Final Form:** Substituting this exact infinite sum back into the exponent of the density from Step 1 directly provides the final analytic representation of $p(X)$, where the manifold's exact volume distortion is entirely encapsulated within the exponential series.

**Q.E.D.**

> **Corollary 2.2: The Hierarchical Truncations of the Manifold Density**
>
> For a particle ensemble on a locally symmetric manifold $(\mathcal{M}, g)$, the infinite-series representation of the exact density $p(X)$ established in Theorem 2.2 admits a hierarchy of Gaussian approximations. To sample a new position $X \in \mathcal{M}$ relative to the consensus point $M$, we draw a tangent vector $v \in T_M\mathcal{M}$ from a zero-mean multivariate normal distribution and project it onto the manifold via the exponential map, such that $X = \text{Exp}_M(v)$. Assuming an orthonormal basis of the tangent space where the metric $g$ acts as the identity matrix $I$, the two primary operational states are:
>
> 1.  **The Euclidean (Zeroth-Order) Approximation ($N < 2$):** Neglecting local geometric distortion, the tangent vector $v$ is generated by an isotropic Gaussian determined strictly by the ratio of the CBO drift $\lambda$ to the noise $\delta$:
      >     $$X = \text{Exp}_M(v), \quad v \sim \mathcal{N}\left( 0, \frac{\delta^2}{2\lambda} I \right)$$
>
> 2.  **The Ricci-Corrected (Second-Order) Approximation ($N = 2$):** Incorporating the leading-order curvature effect, $v$ is generated by a Gaussian whose precision matrix is shifted by the manifold's Ricci curvature tensor $\text{Ric}_M$:
      >     $$X = \text{Exp}_M(v), \quad v \sim \mathcal{N}\left( 0, \left[ \frac{2\lambda}{\delta^2} I + \frac{1}{3} \text{Ric}_M \right]^{-1} \right)$$
>

**Proof.**

1.  **The Generative Pushforward Equivalence:** Theorem 2.2 establishes the exact density $p(X)$ relative to the flat Lebesgue measure of the tangent space, mapping the distance to the tangent vector norm $d_g(X, M) = \|v\|_g$. Because this measure is intrinsically flat, generating a random particle $X$ that obeys $p(X)$ is mathematically equivalent to generating a random tangent vector $v$ from a corresponding flat density $q(v)$, and then pushing it onto the manifold via the exact inverse mapping $X = \text{Exp}_M(v)$. Substituting $v$ into the exponent of Theorem 2.2 defines the exact target density in the tangent space:
    $$q(v) \propto \exp\left( -\frac{\lambda}{\delta^2} \|v\|_g^2 + \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{m=2}^{N} \frac{1}{m!} \nabla_v^m \psi(0) \right)$$

2.  **The Euclidean (Zeroth-Order) Approximation ($N < 2$):** To construct a tractable sampling distribution, we apply truncations to the infinite series of the log-Jacobian $\psi(v)$. For the zeroth-order approximation, we assume the manifold is locally flat, implying the geometric volume distortion is negligible. Truncating at $N < 2$ forces the summation term to vanish, leaving only the primary CBO drift potential. The density simplifies to $q_0(v) \propto \exp\left(-\frac{\lambda}{\delta^2} \|v\|_g^2\right)$. By matching this quadratic form to the standard multivariate normal kernel $\exp\left(-\frac{1}{2} v^\top \Sigma^{-1} v\right)$, we identify the precision matrix as $\Sigma^{-1} = \frac{2\lambda}{\delta^2} I$. Inverting this matrix yields the isotropic covariance $\frac{\delta^2}{2\lambda} I$. Therefore, we sample $v \sim \mathcal{N}\left(0, \frac{\delta^2}{2\lambda} I\right)$ and map it via $X = \text{Exp}_M(v)$.
3.  **The Ricci-Corrected (Second-Order) Approximation ($N = 2$):** To capture the leading-order curvature effects, we truncate the series at $N=2$. In Riemannian normal coordinates, the second derivative of the log-Jacobian evaluated at the origin is strictly determined by the Ricci curvature tensor, satisfying $\frac{1}{2} \nabla_v^2 \psi(0) = -\frac{1}{6} \text{Ric}_M(v, v)$. Substituting this geometric identity into $q(v)$ merges the drift and curvature terms into a single composite quadratic form:
    $$q_2(v) \propto \exp\left( -\left[ \frac{\lambda}{\delta^2} \|v\|_g^2 + \frac{1}{6} \text{Ric}_M(v, v) \right] \right)$$

4.  **Precision Matrix Shift:** Factoring out the standard Gaussian coefficient of $\frac{1}{2}$ from the exponent reveals the shifted precision matrix $\Sigma^{-1} = \frac{2\lambda}{\delta^2} I + \frac{1}{3} \text{Ric}_M$. This demonstrates that the manifold's local geometry acts as a direct modifier to the ensemble's precision, tightening or widening the distribution depending on the curvature. The position $X$ is therefore generated by sampling $v$ from this shifted Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma)$ and applying the exponential pushforward $X = \text{Exp}_M(v)$.

**Q.E.D.**

> **Remark 2.1: The Dimensional Paradox of the Ricci Correction**
>
> While the Ricci-corrected Gaussian ($N=2$) provides a highly accurate tangent-space sampling distribution for low dimensions, it exhibits an artificial collapse as $d \to \infty$. Specifically, the precision shift $\frac{1}{3}\text{Ric}_M \propto d$ implies the covariance approaches zero, seemingly yielding a Dirac measure. This contradicts the concentration of measure phenomenon, which dictates that high-dimensional distributions on compact manifolds increasingly resemble broad Gaussians. This paradox arises because the $N=2$ truncation of the infinite volume distortion series does not commute with the high-dimensional limit.

> **Lemma 2.3: Generalized Dimensional Validity and the Low-Temperature Limit**
>
> Let $(\mathcal{M}, g)$ be a locally symmetric manifold of dimension $D$. For any tangent vector $v$ drawn from the Euclidean baseline approximation $\mathcal{N}\left( 0, \frac{\delta^2}{2\lambda} I \right)$, the second-order Ricci-corrected approximation remains mathematically valid only if the noise amplitude $\delta^2$ scales inversely with the scalar curvature $R_{scal}$ of the manifold.

**Proof.**

1. **Stability Evaluation**: We evaluate the stability of the Maclaurin expansion established in Theorem 2.2 by analyzing the expected magnitude of the second-order geometric correction term. The validity of truncating the infinite series $\psi(v)$ at $N=2$ strictly requires that the leading-order curvature term $\frac{1}{2}\nabla_v^2 \psi(0)$ does not dominate the algorithmic potential. A dominant second-order term implies that uncalculated higher-order derivatives in the series are also non-negligible.
2. **Tangent Vector Expectation**: Under the baseline CBO dynamics, the tangent vector $v$ is distributed according to an isotropic Gaussian with covariance $\Sigma = \frac{\delta^2}{2\lambda} I$. The expected squared norm of this vector in a $D$-dimensional tangent space is determined by the trace of the covariance matrix, yielding $\mathbb{E}[\|v\|_g^2] = D \frac{\delta^2}{2\lambda}$.
3. **Curvature Term Expectation**: From the established identity for locally symmetric spaces, the second derivative of the log-Jacobian at the origin is given by $-\frac{1}{6} \text{Ric}_M(v, v)$. To find the expected influence of this term on the ensemble, we calculate its expectation over the Gaussian draw: $\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{6} \text{Ric}_M(v, v)\right] = \frac{1}{6} \text{Tr}(\text{Ric}_M \Sigma)$.
4. **Simplification**: Substituting the isotropic covariance $\Sigma = \frac{\delta^2}{2\lambda} I$, this expectation simplifies to $\frac{\delta^2}{12\lambda} \text{Tr}(\text{Ric}_M)$.
5. **Scalar Curvature Substitution**: By definition, the trace of the Ricci tensor is the scalar curvature $R_{scal}$ of the manifold. Thus, the expected magnitude of the geometric correction scales exactly as $\frac{\delta^2 R_{scal}}{12\lambda}$.
6. **Divergence and Scaling**: If the manifold's curvature or dimensionality is increased such that $R_{scal}$ diverges—as seen in the $SO(d)$ case where the scalar curvature grows at a rate of $\mathcal{O}(d^3)$—the second-order term will diverge if the noise amplitude $\delta^2$ is kept as a fixed constant.
7. **Conclusion**: Such a divergence renders the $N=2$ truncation invalid, as the infinite sequence of higher-order curvature terms $\mathcal{O}(\|v\|_g^4)$ becomes necessary to counteract the collapse and balance the probability density. Consequently, the Ricci-corrected Gaussian must be viewed as a low-temperature approximation. To maintain a valid probability measure in high-dimensional or high-curvature regimes, the CBO noise amplitude must be specifically scaled such that $\delta^2 \sim \mathcal{O}(R_{scal}^{-1})$.

**Q.E.D.**

This series provides the exact geometric equilibrium of the ensemble, accounting for the global topological constraints of the manifold without reliance on local Taylor approximations.
